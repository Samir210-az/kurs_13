🃏 Metaphoric Associative Cards — Diagnostic and Correction Program (MAC)

An 8-session program — a card-based diagnostic and correction tool that, using a projective method, opens access to a child's/teenager's subconscious emotional material.

1

Historical/Clinical Context

Origin: Metaphoric Associative Cards (MAC) were created in 1975 by Israeli artist Eli Raman and German psychotherapist Moritz Egetmeyer under the name "OH Cards." The method is considered a modern, flexible successor to projective tests (Rorschach, TAT) — instead of standardized shapes, multi-meaning visual images allow the client to project their own life experience. Today it's used by therapists in over 100 countries around the world and finds application in clinical, educational, and even corporate contexts.
Theoretical foundation: The method rests on psychoanalytic projection theory (the client's subconscious material being 'transferred' onto an external object) and narrative therapy's story-based approach. MAC's advantage lies in its flexibility — there's no fixed 'right answer,' each client creates their own unique meaning.

I choose this method most often when working with teenagers and adults who tend to shut down in a direct question-answer format. The power of the cards lies in this: the client can speak without fear of 'giving the wrong answer' — after all, the card itself 'says' nothing, the meaning arises entirely from the client's own interpretation. The method is especially good at opening a fresh door for clients who are tired of direct interviews, who have the feeling of 'I've already said everything' — the multi-meaning nature of the card leads to a different interpretation each time, and thus to a different topic.

2

Purpose

The purpose of MAC cards isn't to make a diagnosis, but to create a new, safe entry point into the client's own inner world — going where the classic interview format can't reach.

MAC serves 3 core purposes:

🔍

Diagnostic

Surfacing emotional material that's hard to express directly in words.

💬

Therapeutic

Safely exploring sensitive topics through metaphoric distance.

🌱

Correction

Uncovering and reinforcing inner resources and strengths.

These 3 purposes can even work sequentially within a single session — diagnostic observation that starts with a card often flows into therapeutic conversation, then into correction work, all in a natural flow, without artificial transitions.

3

Structure (Deck and Sections)

The composition of the deck is chosen according to which client group the therapist is working with — more concrete images are preferred when working with children, more abstract ones when working with adults.

📦 Deck Composition

A standard MAC deck consists of 80-160 cards, usually covering abstract/symbolic images (landscapes, people, objects, abstract shapes). Well-known decks include "OH Cards," "Personita," "Habitat," "COPE" (for children).

🗂️ Sub-decks

Some manufacturers offer theme-oriented sub-decks: feeling cards, family/relationship cards, resource/strength cards — these can be used together with the core deck or separately. The therapist can pre-select and keep ready a combination of several sub-decks suited to their own clinical population.

4

Administrator Requirements

Required qualification Clinical psychologist or psychotherapist

Additional certification Training in projective methods + a specialized MAC certification course (usually 20-40 hours)

Experience requirement Supervised application experience is recommended, especially when working with child/teenage populations

Important note: MAC's results alone aren't sufficient for making a diagnosis — they must be used together with other clinical tools (interviews, behavioral observation, standardized tests). I myself never use this method as the "only" tool — it's always part of a broader assessment process, not a free-standing addition, but a piece of the whole picture.
5

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

Although MAC cards might seem at first glance like "just a psychological tool," in practice they reveal different information through the eyes of 5 different fields.

🧠 Psychological Perspective

As a projective method, MAC allows access to subconscious material by bypassing the client's defense mechanisms — especially useful with resistant clients.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

The card-selection process also engages fine motor and visual-perceptual skills, which can provide additional diagnostic information, especially in young children.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

The visual richness of the cards allows observing which images catch the attention of children with different sensory processing.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The MAC process engages narrative skills, vocabulary, and the ability to express abstract thought.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

Applying MAC in a teacher-student context (in simplified form) is possible in self-awareness and emotional literacy lessons.

6

Clinical Case: Gunay's Story

Gunay was 13 years old, her parents complained "she doesn't talk about anything, she just shrugs." The classic question-answer format wasn't working — the question "how are you?" always got the answer "I'm fine." But when the MAC cards were laid out on the table, something different happened: Gunay lingered over one card for a long time, then said "this reminds me of my mom" — for the first time, she'd shared something of herself.

Assessment Test: MAC Cards (Diagnostic Application)

ℹ️ About the test

A set of multi-meaning photographic cards in use since 1975, working on a projective principle similar to Rorschach, but less threatening.

🎯 Why it's given

To create access to emotional material through metaphoric distance in clients (like Gunay) who don't open up in a direct question-answer format.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist trained in projective methods; interpretation must always be done together with clinical observation, with great caution.

🧰 Materials used

A standard MAC deck (40-160 cards), a quiet table environment, a session note sheet.

As in Gunay's example, the outcome of MAC is never in the form of a "test score" — the value is hidden in the story the client forms in their own words.

Recommendations for Parents

Work with MAC cards is especially sensitive with teenagers — the recommendations below explain to the parent how to support the process while respecting the client's confidentiality.

🔒 Protect confidentiality

What your child shares with the cards can be sensitive — respect the trusting relationship with the therapist, without requiring it to be shared with you.

🚫 Don't look for right/wrong

There's no "right answer" in MAC — don't evaluate your child's card choices, just support the process.

🤝 Be open to the family session

A family MAC session helps the family see each other from different perspectives — participate with an open mind.

10

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Unexpected material surfacing when working with metaphoric cards

Due to the projective nature of MAC (Metaphoric Associative Cards), emotional defenses can be bypassed 'through a side door,' surfacing strong material at an unexpected speed:

  • The child suddenly crying, going silent, or wanting to leave the room after choosing a card;
  • Details very close to a real traumatic event emerging in the explanation of the chosen card;
  • Emotional 'flooding' — the child losing their own regulation ability (rapid breathing, trembling).
  1. During a strong reaction, immediately stop opening additional cards — containment is the priority, not continuing.
  2. Switch to grounding technique: breath regulation, seeing/naming 5 objects.
  3. Don't close the session until the child calms down, stabilize the emotional state before handing them back to the parent.
  4. If a real trauma detail is uncovered, begin the reporting procedure in accordance with the law.
  5. Before returning to that card's topic in the next session, gently check whether the child is ready — don't force it.

📌 Topic Summary

  • MAC, in use since 1975, is a flexible, modern form of projective methods.
  • The method combines diagnostic, therapeutic, and correction functions — surfacing not only problems, but also resources.
  • The structured 4-stage procedure (open choice → theme-oriented → deepening questions → integration) should be followed.
  • The results are a qualitative analysis, not a quantitative score, and should be interpreted together with other clinical information.
