Topic 06 · Family System

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Adolescents and Family Relationships

An 8-session program — a structured path that strengthens teen-parent communication and maintains the balance between boundaries and independence.

1

Scientific Definition: Family Systems Theory

Clinical definition: According to Bowen's Family Systems Theory (1978), a family is not a collection of separate individuals but a system in mutual interaction — one member's behavior directly affects the others. Steinberg's (2001) theory of adolescent individuation shows that healthy teen development requires both autonomy and the continuation of emotional attachment to the family. According to Baumrind's (1991) parenting styles model, the "authoritative" (firm-and-warm) approach — combining clear boundaries with warmth — produces the healthiest outcomes.
In simple terms: A family can be compared to an orchestra — each musician (family member) plays their own part, but together they create a symphony. When one musician changes their note, it affects the whole orchestra. During adolescence, the 'musician' wants to find their own voice, but still wants to remain part of the orchestra — this program helps find that balance.

The program integrates Gottman's communication model, Bowen's family systems theory, and Baumrind's parenting styles model. In my experience, family sessions require a different skill than individual sessions — the therapist must never create the impression that one side is 'right' and the other 'wrong.' Both sides need to leave the room feeling 'heard,' otherwise the program's effect fades very quickly.

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

Within the framework of family systems theory, a teenager's behavior should be assessed in the context of family dynamics, not as an isolated problem.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Each family member's sensory-motor preferences can be taken into account when choosing shared activities (games, handicrafts) so that the activity is accessible to everyone.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

During high-tension family moments, calming pause strategies (leaving the room, breathing) prevent conflict escalation.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The 'I-language' formula, within the framework of pragmatic language skills, presents a concrete linguistic structure for non-accusatory communication.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

Family-school collaboration (homework rules, etc.) unifies the teenager's academic and emotional support system.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early adolescence (11-13 years)

  • The demand for autonomy is still relatively low, parental authority is strong
  • Concrete, visual communication tools (cards) are effective
  • Family time is still a high priority for the teenager

📚 Middle adolescence (14-16 years)

  • The demand for autonomy peaks, boundary discussions intensify
  • Peer influence competes with parental influence
  • Structured communication tools like 'I-language' are especially useful

🎓 Late adolescence (17+ years)

  • Family relationships shift toward a more 'equal' form
  • New areas of discussion arise around future plans (university)
  • Trust and independent decision-making are central topics
5

Core Model: A 4-Pillar Approach

The program focuses on 4 core pillars of family relationships:

⚖️ Autonomy-Attachment Balance

Based on Steinberg's individuation theory, supporting independence and closeness in parallel.

💬 Effective Communication

Gottman's 'I-language' and active listening principles.

🤝 Conflict Resolution

Replacing harmful patterns (criticism, contempt) with healthy alternatives.

🔑 Building Trust

Building and repairing trust through consistent small honest actions.

Clinical Case: The Story of Elvin's Family

Elvin was 14 years old when his mother, Aygun Khanim, said in the first call, "we can't talk normally with him anymore, every conversation ends in an argument." But speaking separately with Elvin himself, I saw a different picture — he loved his family, he just constantly felt 'under control.' When I started working with both of them, I understood that looking for someone 'guilty' here was pointless — simply an outdated communication pattern needed to adapt to a new developmental stage. This is a very common situation in family therapy: a parenting style that worked perfectly during childhood becomes outdated once the teenager begins to see themselves as a separate person, and both sides need new 'rules.'

Assessment Test: FACES-IV (Family Adaptability and Cohesion Evaluation Scale)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Olson (2010), a standard tool measuring a family's level of adaptability and cohesion, filled out separately by each family member.

🎯 Why it's given

To objectively determine whether the family's current dynamic is 'balanced' or 'extreme' (either overly rigid or overly chaotic).

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist trained in family therapy; results are interpreted by comparing each family member's forms.

🧰 Materials used

A separate questionnaire for each family member, a scoring chart, a comparative graph of results.

As in the example of Elvin's family, often the most valuable information isn't in the numbers themselves, but in how much the two forms differ from each other — that difference alone already shows which direction we need to work with the family in.

Recommendations for Parents

In family therapy, change shouldn't stay only in the session room — as we saw in the example of Elvin and Aygun Khanim, real progress happens in small daily moments, for example at the dinner table.

💬 Use 'I-language'

Speak in the form of "I feel that..." instead of "You always..." — this reduces defensive reactions.

⚖️ Make room for autonomy

Let the teenager make some decisions — this strengthens both their self-esteem and their connection with you.

🕰️ Set aside regular family time

Once a week, phone-free shared activity significantly strengthens relationships.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Risk signals within the family

When working on family relationships, the therapist must monitor not only communication problems, but also serious risk factors that may be hidden underneath:

  • Attempts to run away from home, or repeated talk about it;
  • Signs of physical violence, controlling/threatening behavior patterns from a parent;
  • Signs of active substance dependence in a parent and the child's exposure to it;
  • Signs of neglect — consistently coming hungry, clothing not suited to the season, unmet medical needs.
  1. Create an opportunity to speak with each member separately and confidentially during the family session — topics of violence are not disclosed in front of the family.
  2. If suspicion of neglect/violence is confirmed, immediately fulfill your obligation to report to the relevant authorities.
  3. In cases with a high risk of running away from home, draw up a concrete safety plan (who they can contact, where they can go, emergency numbers).
  4. In a moment of sharp conflict between parent and child, move directly into a 'de-escalation' role, pause the session, and set aside individual calming time.

📌 Topic Summary

  • Family relationships function as a system — a change in one member affects the whole family (Bowen).
  • Healthy adolescent development requires both autonomy and family attachment (Steinberg).
  • 'I-language' and active listening form the basis of non-accusatory, effective communication.
  • Trust is built and repaired through consistent small honest actions, while regular family time strengthens attachment.
