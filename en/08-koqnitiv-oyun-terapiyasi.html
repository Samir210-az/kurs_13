Topic 08 · Play Therapy

🧩 Cognitive Play Therapy

An 8-session program — a structured model integrating cognitive-behavioral principles into a play format.

1

Scientific Definition: Foundations of CBT Play Therapy

Clinical definition: Cognitive-Behavioral Play Therapy (Knell, 1993) integrates the principles of classic Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (Beck, 1979) — the thought-feeling-behavior link, cognitive restructuring, behavioral experiments — into a play format. Since young children don't yet have fully abstract thinking ability (Piaget's stages of cognitive development), cognitive concepts need to be presented through puppets, games, and concrete metaphors.
In simple terms: Telling an adult "question your thoughts" can work, but for a young child this feels abstract. But if we say, "Let's be detectives and look for evidence for this thought," the same cognitive process suddenly turns into a fun game, and the child participates eagerly.

The program is especially effective for ages 4-10 and can be applied to a wide range of difficulties like negative self-perception, aggression, and anxiety. The power of puppet characters like Cookie lies in this: the child can test their own thought without fear of being criticized — the puppet can 'make a mistake,' which allows the child to learn without becoming defensive. I see this difference again every time: the same question, asked directly, is met with silence, but asked through a puppet, it's met with an open answer.

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

Cognitive work with a young child is never the work of a single field alone — 5 specialist perspectives often overlap in the same session, frequently within the same game.

🧠 Psychological Perspective

The cognitive restructuring process strengthens the child's self-regulation and problem-solving ability, increasing resilience to future emotional difficulties.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Puzzle and LEGO activities present the problem-solving cognitive process in a concrete, hands-on form, in parallel with fine motor skills.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

A physical ritual (placing a hand on the heart) strengthens the sensory-motor connection when activating the super power words, making them easier to recall.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The process of expressing thoughts in words helps the child shape their inner monologue with more developed language skills.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

Detective questions and problem-solving steps are transferable skills that can also be applied to academic difficulties in the classroom setting.

4

Age Groups

The level of abstraction of cognitive concepts is directly tied to age — at a young age, everything can be worked through puppets and concrete metaphor, while at an older age, direct conversation becomes possible.

👶 Early age (4-6 years)

  • Puppets are used as the main tool, abstract explanations are kept to a minimum
  • Visual, colorful materials are important for holding attention
  • Sessions should be kept shorter (20-30 min)

📚 Middle age (7-9 years)

  • Detective questions and cognitive restructuring are applied more independently
  • Written elements (cards) can be added
  • Behavioral experiments are planned more freely in real life

🎓 Older age (10+ years)

  • The play element decreases, a transition to direct cognitive discussion becomes possible
  • The child can independently recognize their own cognitive distortions
  • The 'superhero' metaphor can be adapted to different role models for teenagers

Core Model: CBT-Play Integration

The program combines classic CBT components with play elements:

🔺 Cognitive Triangle (Puppet)

A visual presentation of the thought-feeling-behavior link.

🔍 Detective Questions

A gamified form of Beck's cognitive restructuring principle.

🧩 Problem-Solving Puzzles

Emotional problem-solving worked in parallel with solving a physical puzzle.

🦸 Super Power Words

An adaptation of Meichenbaum's self-instruction model into children's language.

Clinical Case: Elnur's Story

Elnur was 7 years old when his parents referred him, and the main complaint was "calling himself stupid at every small failure" — when a drawing came out crooked, when he lost a game, even when he misspelled a word, he'd immediately say "I can't do anything." A classic CBT conversation didn't yet fully work at his age, but when the same ideas came up with a puppet named Cookie, Elnur suddenly turned into an interested observer — he began seeing his own thought from the outside. This difference isn't accidental: 7-year-olds haven't yet fully entered the abstract thinking stage (according to Piaget, they're in the concrete-operational stage), so the task "question your own thought" stays too abstract, but "question Cookie's thought" turns into a concrete, hands-on task.

Assessment Test: SCARED (Screen for Child Anxiety Related Disorders)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Birmaher and colleagues (1997), a 41-item screening tool for anxiety-related emotional disorders, with both child and parent versions.

🎯 Why it's given

To determine whether there's a hidden anxiety component underlying negative self-evaluation, and to fine-tune the intervention accordingly.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist; self-report is used for children over 8, while for younger children only the parent version is applied.

🧰 Materials used

A standard question sheet (child/parent version), a scoring key, a results table across 5 subscales.

As in Elnur's example, screening results in the young age group should always be interpreted together with behavior observed during play — a single number can never replace the child's full picture.

Recommendations for Parents

At a young age, cognitive skills strengthen through repetition. For children like Elnur, consistent, gamified repetition at home ensures that the skill learned in the clinic truly 'sinks in' — a single session is never enough.

🔍 Ask the detective questions together

When your child expresses a negative thought, gently ask "What's the evidence for this?"

🦸 Remind them of the super power words

In a hard moment, you can gently remind your child of the 'power word' they learned.

🧩 Combine problem-solving with play

Approaching real problems like a puzzle (plan-try-check) makes learning easier for your child.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Sharp escalation of fear/anxiety and risk of disclosure

In Cognitive Play Therapy, structured tasks sometimes trigger unexpectedly strong emotional reactions or disclosures:

  • An anxiety episode reaching panic level (shortness of breath, crying, wanting to run) during the session;
  • It becoming clear that a symbol presented as the 'fear object' is actually a real person/event;
  • The child speaking self-punishingly ("I'm bad," "it's my fault") in a disproportionate way.
  1. During an acute anxiety episode, immediately stop the cognitive task and switch to breathing and grounding techniques.
  2. When the real source behind a symbolic 'fear object' becomes clear, gather further information calmly and without judgment.
  3. If the disclosure is of a violent/abusive nature, begin the reporting procedure.
  4. A pattern of excessive self-blame should be cross-checked with a mood screening from the Depression/Grief module — comorbidity should be assessed.

  • CBT Play Therapy integrates classic cognitive-behavioral principles into an accessible, play-based format for young children.
  • The cognitive triangle (thought-feeling-behavior) forms the conceptual foundation of the program.
  • 'Detective questions' allow negative thoughts to be questioned in a fun, systematic way.
  • Problem-solving puzzles and 'super power words' support the consistent, independent application of the skills learned.
