Topic 03 · Solution-Focused Model

🌟 Kids'Skills Program

Ben Furman's Kids'Skills method — a 15-step, play-based approach that turns a child's difficulty into a "learnable skill."

1

Scientific Definition: The Basics of the Kids'Skills Method

Clinical definition: Kids'Skills (Furman, 2004) is a structured, 15-step method based on the principles of Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (de Shazer). The method's core principle is the "externalization" of the problem (influenced by White & Epston's narrative therapy): the child's difficulty is presented not as part of their character, but as a separate, learnable "skill." This prevents the child from identifying with their problem and strengthens the belief "I can learn" (self-efficacy, Bandura).
In simple terms: Imagine, instead of telling a child "You're a coward," we say "You're learning the skill of being brave." It's as if we're lifting the problem off the child's shoulders and placing it in front of them as a "task to be learned" — like passing a new level in a video game.

Kids'Skills is a play- and creativity-based method, applied through the collaboration of parents, teachers, and therapists, where the child actively takes ownership of their own process.

Areas Where the Method Can Be Applied

😨

Fear and Anxiety

Fear of the dark, fear of being alone, specific phobias.

E.g.: the skill of sleeping alone.

🚽

Daily Habits

Independence skills like toilet training, dressing, brushing teeth.

E.g.: the skill of dressing oneself.

😤

Emotional Regulation

Self-regulation difficulties like aggression, anger outbursts, impatience.

E.g.: the skill of staying calm.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological perspective

The method is grounded in self-efficacy and intrinsic motivation theories — the child taking ownership of their own process supports long-term behavior change.

✋ Occupational therapist's perspective

When the skill has a physical/motor component (e.g. dressing), step-by-step task analysis can be combined with the method.

🌊 Sensory integration perspective

When choosing the skill's "symbol" or "mascot," the child's sensory preferences (touch, visual) should be considered so the practice materials are more effective.

🗣️ Speech-language pathologist's perspective

The process of shaping the skill's name should be tailored to the child's language development level, using simple and concrete expressions.

🏫 Psycho-pedagogical perspective

Engaging the teacher as a "team member" ensures the skill is also supported in the classroom environment.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early childhood (ages 3-6)

  • The method was originally designed for this age group
  • Visual elements (drawings, cardstock) are especially effective
  • Parent participation is critical at this age

📚 School age (ages 7-11)

  • The child can engage in the process more independently
  • Written elements (letters, charts) can be added
  • Peer helpers (friends) can be included on the team

🎓 Adolescence (12+)

  • The method can be adapted for more abstract skills (stress management)
  • The adolescent's own motivation is decisive for the process's success
  • The "mascot" concept can shift into a "role model" form for adolescents
5

Core Model: Furman's 15 Steps

The Kids'Skills method consists of 15 sequential steps; in this program, each step is applied as a separate session:

1️⃣ Naming the Skill

Turning the problem into a learnable skill and giving it a name.

2️⃣ Understanding the Benefits

Identifying the concrete life benefits of the skill.

3️⃣ An Appealing Name

Tailoring the name to the child's world (characters).

4️⃣ Choosing the Team

Identifying the people who will help.

5️⃣ Detailed Description

Vividly picturing having fully mastered the skill.

6️⃣ Self-Confidence

Strengthening belief by remembering past successes.

7️⃣ Asking for Help

Formally asking team members for help.

8️⃣-9️⃣ Celebration and Announcement

Planning how to mark the success and telling everyone.

🔟-1️⃣2️⃣ Practice and Tracking

Role-play practice, a reminder system, and tracking progress.

1️⃣3️⃣-1️⃣5️⃣ Completion

Celebrating success, expressing thanks, and passing the skill on to someone else.

Clinical Case: Nihad's Story

Nihad was 8 years old; his mother spoke a bit shyly at the first meeting — her son still came to his parents' room every night to sleep with them, and cried when trying to stay alone in his own room, saying "the shadows will get me." When I listened to this story, the first thing I thought wasn't "What's wrong with Nihad?" but "Which skill has Nihad not yet learned?" — the entire philosophy of the Kids'Skills method is built on exactly this question. As Furman emphasizes, a child is rarely "lazy" or "spoiled"; usually they simply haven't mastered a particular skill yet, and it's our job to teach it to them.

SectionNotes (Nihad's example)
Presenting problemInability to sleep alone in his own room, coming to his parents' bed every night, fear of the dark.
Precipitating factorsAccidentally witnessing a scary movie scene recently, attention being divided by a younger sibling's birth in the family.
Predisposing factorsA naturally cautious temperament, parents previously always taking him in with them during moments of fear.
Perpetuating factorsGoing to the parents' room instantly reduces the fear, which reinforces the behavior; phrases like "you're too old to be scared" have the opposite effect.
Protective factorsHis creative imagination (which can be both a source of fear and a resource), a warm relationship with his family, enthusiasm for learning new things.
Core belief"I can't be alone by myself, someone must always be with me" — an early schema related to self-confidence.
Treatment goalReframing the fear not as a 'problem' but as a learnable 'Brave Sleeping Skill,' turning Nihad into the active hero of the process.

A short excerpt from a therapist-client dialogue (from step 2, while naming the skill):

SpeakerLine
TherapistNihad, if being able to sleep alone were a superhero power, what would you name that power?
NihadI don't know... maybe "Brave Night Hero"?
TherapistThat's a fantastic name! What superpowers would the Brave Night Hero have?
NihadHe wouldn't be afraid of the dark, because he'd know there's nothing there.
TherapistYou already have a piece of that power — do you know how I know? Because you came up with this name yourself.
NihadReally? (smiles with a bit of pride)
TherapistYes! Now let's practice this power in small little steps — starting today.
📝 Homework example (for Nihad): Every night before bed, whisper the name "Brave Night Hero" to himself 3 times, and try sitting alone for 5 minutes in his room with the night light on — regardless of the outcome, simply write down the attempt on the star chart.

Assessment Tool: Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Goodman (1997), a 25-item, widely used brief behavioral-emotional screening tool. It has 5 subscales (emotional symptoms, conduct problems, hyperactivity, peer problems, prosocial behavior).

🎯 Why it's given

To get a picture of the child's overall functioning before applying Kids'Skills, to determine whether attention is needed beyond just the target skill.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist or a specialist in child development; parent and teacher forms are filled out in parallel and compared.

🧰 Materials used

Standard SDQ question sheet (parent/teacher version), scoring key, chart for recording results.

Recommendations for Parents

🚫 Avoid the word "problem"

When talking about your child's difficulty, use the phrase "is learning a skill" instead of saying "has a problem".

🎉 Celebrate small wins

Don't wait for the skill to be fully mastered — praise and note every small step.

👥 Be a team member

When your child asks you for help, take it seriously and provide concrete, consistent support.

10

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Situations requiring special attention in the stuttering program

Kids'Skills is a strength-focused, gamified method, but this doesn't mean every "problem" is simply a skill gap. In the following situations, the method should be stopped immediately and a different protocol followed:

  • Suspicion of violence/abuse behind a behavior presented as a "problem" (e.g. bedwetting, withdrawal, aggression);
  • The child's 'failure' to learn a skill actually being a trauma reaction (freezing, hypervigilance);
  • A parent applying the skill task in a punitive, shaming way.
  1. Before every new "skill project," check the origin of the problem with a brief structured question — don't just accept the symptom and move forward.
  2. If suspicion of abuse/violence arises, stop the Kids'Skills process immediately and switch to a direct assessment protocol.
  3. If the suspicion is confirmed, fulfill the legally mandated duty to report, and work separately with the parent.
  4. If a trauma reaction is suspected, adapt the method to a trauma-informed framework, or refer in parallel to a trauma specialist.

📌 Topic Summary

  • The Kids'Skills method turns a problem into a "learnable skill," giving the child ownership of their own process.
  • The 15 steps are applied sequentially: from naming, to building a team, practicing, celebrating, and finally passing the skill on to someone else.
  • The method is especially effective with parent and teacher involvement — the social support network is the key to success.
  • The final step, based on the "learning by teaching" principle, fully reinforces the skill and also gives the child a gain in empathy.
