Topic 11 · Play Therapy

🔷 Play Therapy with Children on the Autism Spectrum

An 8-session program — the DIR/Floortime model, which develops social-communicative skills in children on the Autism Spectrum through play.

1

Scientific Definition: Foundations of the DIR/Floortime Model

Clinical definition: DIR/Floortime (Developmental, Individual-differences, Relationship-based model, Stanley Greenspan & Serena Wieder, 1990s) is a relationship-centered intervention model for working with children on the autism spectrum, based on the principle of "following the child's lead." D (Developmental) covers the child's functional-emotional developmental stage, I (Individual differences) covers each child's unique sensory-motor profile, and R (Relationship-based) covers the principle that development happens through relationships. The model focuses on the gradual development of joint attention, symbolic play, and reciprocal communication.
In simple terms: Floortime literally means "floor time" — the adult gets down to the child's level, sits with them on the floor, and 'joins' the play the child is doing, instead of pulling the child into their own play. It's like learning to speak the child's language — first we enter their world, then we gradually build a shared language.

This program presents a family-centered, play-based approach to support the social-communicative development of children on the autism spectrum. What sets DIR/Floortime apart from other behavior-based approaches is that the goal isn't to 'teach' concrete behaviors, but to develop the capacity for connection itself. When applying this model, I often tell parents that the measure of success isn't big changes — a single second of extra eye contact, even one shared sincere smile, is already a very valuable, big step.

2

Core Areas of Development

These areas don't develop sequentially but in parallel — progress in one area naturally, indirectly strengthens the others too.

👀

Joint Attention

Two people sharing attention on the same object — the foundation of social communication.

E.g.: pointing, triangular gaze.

🎭

Symbolic Play

The transition from functional play to pretend/symbolic play.

E.g.: 'drinking' from an empty cup.

🔄

Reciprocal Communication

Turn-taking, sharing, and mutual social exchange.

E.g.: a ball-passing game.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

The relationship-centered approach supports the child's emotional development in parallel with social-communicative skills.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Assessing each child's unique sensory-motor profile (I — Individual differences) is the foundation for personalizing the intervention.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

Choosing materials suited to the child's sensory preferences (movement, touch, sound) makes it easier to build connection.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

Joint attention and symbolic play are the earliest, foundational stages of language development — this program supports pre-verbal communication skills.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

The Social Stories method is a visual-structured teaching tool that can also be applied in the school environment.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early age (2-5 years)

  • The most intensively researched age group for Floortime
  • The transition from joint attention to functional-symbolic play is the priority
  • Parent participation and daily application are critically important

📚 Middle age (6-10 years)

  • Social Stories and structured social skills training are added
  • Sibling participation enriches social exchange
  • Transfer to the school environment becomes a priority

🎓 Older age (11+ years)

  • More complex social scenarios (friendship, group activity) are added
  • Self-awareness and self-advocacy skills are developed
  • Areas of interest (special interests) can be used to teach social skills
5

Core Model: The 3 Components of DIR/Floortime

The program integrates the 3 components of Greenspan & Wieder's model:

D — Developmental

Goals aligned with the child's functional-emotional developmental stage (joint attention, reciprocal communication).

I — Individual Differences

An approach adapted to each child's unique sensory-motor profile.

R — Relationship-based

Development happening through warm, relationship-centered mutual interaction.

📖 Social Stories

Explaining concrete social situations in visual-structured form (Carol Gray).

Clinical Case: Orkhan's Story

Orkhan was 5 years old, diagnosed on the autism spectrum at age 3. His parents were mainly concerned that he didn't 'play together' with his sister Zeynab — even when Orkhan was in the room, he stayed in his own world, eye contact was brief, joint attention was almost absent. This is exactly where DIR/Floortime's strength lies: I didn't try to 'fix' Orkhan, I joined his play — wherever he was, I went there too. In a separate conversation with Zeynab herself, she also had her own feelings — sometimes it was hard for her that her brother 'didn't see her.' That's why including the sister in the program was important not just for Orkhan, but for the whole family system, because the sibling relationship is itself a developmental resource — a two-way bond, not a one-sided one.

📝 Homework example (for Orkhan's family): The parent joining Orkhan's chosen play for 10 minutes every day — without directing, simply entering his world. Zeynab can also voluntarily join part of this time, which is beneficial for both children and considerably strengthens the whole family's bond with each other, mutual understanding, and daily communication quality.

Assessment Test: M-CHAT-R (Autism Screening)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Robins and colleagues, a 20-item parent questionnaire screening for autism risk in children aged 16-30 months.

🎯 Why it's given

To identify early risk signals and refer for a timely, full diagnostic assessment.

🎓 Who can administer it

A pediatrician, clinical psychologist, or developmental specialist; a positive result must always be confirmed with a full diagnostic assessment.

🧰 Materials used

A standard question sheet, an explanatory interview protocol (for positive results), a results chart.

As in Orkhan's example, screening tools are only a starting point — the direction of the real program is determined by the DIR model's functional-emotional stage assessment.

Recommendations for Parents

The effect of DIR/Floortime grows stronger when it extends beyond the session room — for children like Orkhan, short daily 'floor times' create a greater cumulative effect than clinical sessions.

👣 Follow your child's lead

Instead of suggesting your own play, join the activity your child has chosen — this is the foundation of connection.

📖 Use Social Stories

Prepare for new or difficult situations in advance with a simple, visual story.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Involve the whole family

Siblings also learning Floortime principles enriches the child's social world.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Risk of self-injurious behavior (SIB) and elopement

Two serious safety risks are more common when working with children with autism and require a direct protocol:

  • Self-injurious behavior (head-banging, biting, scratching) — a sharp increase in frequency and severity;
  • Elopement/wandering — the child suddenly moving away from an unsupervised environment, especially the risk of being drawn to bodies of water (drowning is a leading cause of death in children with autism);
  • The frequency of self-injurious behavior being directly linked to communication difficulty in functional analysis.
  1. During a SIB episode, ensure physical safety (soft barrier, protecting the head) — don't respond punitively.
  2. Conduct a Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA): what function does the behavior serve — attention, escape, sensory stimulation, or pain?
  3. If SIB frequency/severity increases sharply, refer for a pediatric/medical assessment to rule out a source of pain (teeth, stomach, middle ear) — the behavior isn't always 'psychological.'
  4. Give every family with elopement risk a concrete safety plan: door alarms, a GPS tracking bracelet, informing neighbors/school, swim safety training.
  5. Communication alternatives (PECS, AAC device) should be urgently applied as a replacement function for SIB.

📌 Topic Summary

  • DIR/Floortime supports relationship-centered development through the principle of "following the child's lead."
  • The model is adapted to each child's unique sensory-motor profile (Individual Differences).
  • Joint attention and symbolic play are cornerstones of social-communicative development.
  • Social Stories and family-centered application ensure that the skills learned transfer into daily life.
