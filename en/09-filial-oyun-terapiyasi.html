Topic 09 · Play Therapy

🤝 Filial Play Therapy

An 8-session program — a model (Filial Therapy) in which the parent themselves acquires therapeutic play skills and deepens their connection with their child.

1

Scientific Definition: Foundations of Filial Therapy

Clinical definition: Filial Therapy (Bernard & Louise Guerney, 1964; its modern refined form — Risë VanFleet's Child-Parent Relationship Therapy, CPRT) is a unique model because, instead of the therapist working directly with the child, the parent is trained as the "primary agent of change." The parent is taught the core skills of child-centered play therapy (Virginia Axline, 1947) — tracking, empathic response (reflective listening), and structured limit-setting — so they can run regular 'special playtime' at home.
In simple terms: In many types of therapy, the therapist 'catches the fish' — working directly with the child. Filial Therapy instead 'teaches the parent to fish' — this skill stays permanently, even when the therapist isn't in the room. This is one of the most lasting-effect approaches, because the parent can apply this skill at home every day.

Research (Bratton et al., 2005 meta-analysis) shows that Filial Therapy significantly improves the quality of the parent-child relationship and the child's behavioral problems. What surprised me most the first time I applied this model was the change in the parents — they often said "I've never seen my child this way before," because for the first time they were learning to simply observe instead of correcting. As a therapist, this reminded me too that sometimes the most powerful intervention is doing nothing — just being fully present.

2

3 Core Skills

These 3 skills build on each other — each requires the previous one, changing the order is not recommended.

👁️

Tracking

Describing what the child is doing in words, without judgment.

E.g.: "You picked the red cube."

💗

Empathic Response

Recognizing the child's feelings and reflecting them in words.

E.g.: "You're upset about that."

🚧

Setting Limits

Maintaining safety in 3 steps (acknowledge, state, offer an alternative).

While preserving the child's autonomy.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

Strengthening the parent-child attachment (attachment theory, Bowlby) increases the child's sense of emotional safety and reduces behavioral problems.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Organizing the play room (accessible, safe materials) supports the child's free motor-sensory exploration.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

Child-centered play lets the child choose activities suited to their own sensory needs, which supports self-regulation.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The parent's tracking and empathic response language naturally contributes to the development of the child's speech and emotional vocabulary.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

The listening skills the parent learns can also be applied in the context of homework and academic support.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early age (3-6 years)

  • The most researched age group for Filial Therapy
  • High effectiveness with simple toys (blocks, puppets)
  • The simplicity of the parent's language is especially important

📚 Middle age (7-10 years)

  • More complex play scenarios and stories can emerge
  • The child can express their emotions more independently
  • The limit-setting skill may be needed more often

🎓 Older age (11+ years)

  • The model can be adapted into activity-based shared time (sports, handicrafts)
  • The child-centered listening principle remains the foundation
  • A more conversation-based format may become preferable
5

Core Model: The Guerney-VanFleet Sequence

The program follows the classic Filial Therapy sequence:

📖 Training Phase

Sequentially teaching the parent the 3 core skills (tracking, empathy, limits).

👀 Supervised Practice

The parent running their first sessions under the therapist's observation.

🏠 Home Sessions

Independent, regular weekly 'special playtime.'

🔄 Reinforcement

Resolving emerging difficulties and continuously refining the skills.

6

Clinical Case: The Story of Leyla's Family

Leyla was 5 years old when her mother, Ms. Gunel, appeared very tired at the first meeting — she said, "I don't know how to play with her, I feel like I get it wrong every time." This is exactly where Filial Therapy's difference lies: I didn't work with Leyla, I worked with Ms. Gunel — because real change forms in the small moments that happen at home every day, not in one hour in my room. The first 2 sessions were held with Ms. Gunel alone, without Leyla participating — this surprises many parents, but practicing with the child before learning the skills is like putting an unprepared driver behind the wheel. This waiting period significantly increases the quality of the following sessions, because the parent joins the play with self-confidence, without rushing.

📝 Homework example (for Leyla's family): 3 times a week, 20 minutes of 'special playtime' — during this time Ms. Gunel doesn't ask questions, doesn't correct, just tracks and describes. The phone and other distractions are completely set aside for this time, full attention goes only and entirely to Leyla and the game she chooses.

Assessment Test: Parent-Child Relationship Inventory (PCRI)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Gerard (1994), a self-report relationship quality measure filled out by the parent, consisting of 7 subscales (support, satisfaction, control, etc.).

🎯 Why it's given

To determine, before starting Filial Therapy, which areas of the parent-child relationship need strengthening.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist or family therapist; the results guide which skills the program will focus on more.

🧰 Materials used

A standard question sheet, a scoring key, a profile chart for recording results.

As in Leyla's family's example, this scale is repeated at the start and end of the program, allowing the parent to see for themselves how the relationship has changed in their own eyes.

Recommendations for Parents

In Filial Therapy, the parent is no longer a passive observer, but the main participant in the process — the recommendations below answer the questions parents like Ms. Gunel ask most often.

⏰ Set aside a fixed time

Once a week, 20-30 minutes, uninterrupted 'special playtime' — protect the sanctity of this time.

🎯 Track instead of directing

Instead of suggesting or asking questions, just describe in words what the child is doing.

🚧 Set simple, clear limits

Only set limits for safety and protecting belongings, otherwise give the child freedom.

10

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Parental crisis and risk within the family

Because Filial Therapy turns the parent into a direct participant in the process, sometimes the parent's own crisis also surfaces:

  • Signs of severe parental burnout — statements like "I can't take it anymore," fear of losing control;
  • The parent disclosing that they feel dangerous toward the child;
  • Indirect disclosure of intrafamily violence during filial session coaching.
  1. If the parent says they "feel dangerous," take this seriously and listen without judgment — this is often a call for help.
  2. Immediately activate the parent's individual support resources (family member, friend, professional help), work on a temporary separation plan if needed.
  3. If the child's safety is in doubt, pause the filial coaching and switch directly to an assessment protocol.
  4. If violence is disclosed, take the legally required reporting step.

📌 Topic Summary

  • Filial Therapy trains the parent as the "primary agent of change" — this ensures the effect continues at home.
  • The 3 core skills (tracking, empathic response, limit-setting) form the foundation of the parent-child relationship.
  • The supervised practice stage lets the parent reinforce their skills based on feedback.
  • Regular weekly 'special playtime' becomes the main source of long-term attachment and trust.
