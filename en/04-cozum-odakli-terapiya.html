Topic 04 · Brief Model

🧭 Solution-Focused Brief Therapy for Children & Adolescents

An 8-session Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) program — an approach that focuses not on the details of the problem, but on the desired future.

1

Scientific Definition: The Clinical Basis of SFBT

Clinical definition: Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (de Shazer & Insoo Kim Berg, Milwaukee's Brief Family Therapy Center, 1980s) is a postmodern therapeutic approach that focuses not on cause-and-effect analysis of the problem, but on the client's desired future and existing resources. Core techniques include the Miracle Question, Exception Finding, Scaling Questions, and the strengths-based approach. The model is grounded in the principle that "knowing the details of the problem is not required to solve it."
In simple terms: Imagine you're lost in a city. One approach asks you in detail "how did you get lost," while another asks directly "where do you want to go?" and helps you find the way there together. SFBT is the second approach — instead of investigating the problem, it focuses on finding the path to the desired future.

This model is especially effective in work with children and adolescents because it is brief (5-10 sessions), positive, and future-oriented. In practice I often combine it with other behavior-oriented interventions (such as sensory regulation techniques), because a goal the child forms in their own words creates far stronger motivation than any recommendation given from the outside.

2

SFBT's Core Techniques

✨

The Miracle Question

By describing a state where the problem has been magically solved, concrete details about the solution are uncovered.

E.g.: "If a miracle happened, what would be different tomorrow?"

🔍

Exception Finding

By exploring moments when the problem didn't occur, existing solution resources are uncovered.

E.g.: "Which day were you not angry?"

📏

Scaling Questions

Turning abstract feelings into a measurable form on a 1-10 scale, to track small progress.

E.g.: "Where are you now, from 1 to 10?"

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological perspective

SFBT's resource-focused view strengthens the child's sense of self-efficacy, supporting a shift from problem-centered to solution-centered thinking.

✋ Occupational therapist's perspective

The small-steps principle offers a parallel framework, in line with how motor skills are also taught gradually, in accessible stages.

🌊 Sensory integration perspective

Including sensory details (sound, touch, movement) when describing the miracle day helps the child feel their goal more vividly and concretely.

🗣️ Speech-language pathologist's perspective

The use of positive language (words describing the solution, not the problem) directly influences how the child expresses their own experience.

🏫 Psycho-pedagogical perspective

Teachers applying scaling questions and the small-steps principle in the classroom keeps academic and behavioral goals accessible.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early childhood (ages 4-7)

  • The miracle question is applied in a simplified, drawing-based form
  • Scaling questions are presented visually (a colored ladder)
  • Parent participation makes the process easier

📚 School age (ages 8-12)

  • The child can form their own goals in their own words
  • Written plans (weekly steps) can be added
  • Self-monitoring skills are developed

🎓 Adolescence (13+)

  • The model can be applied to more abstract goals (motivation, relationships)
  • Respecting the adolescent's own agenda is central to the process
  • The brief format preserves the adolescent's sense of autonomy
5

Core Model: SFBT's Structured Sequence

The program follows de Shazer and Berg's classic SFBT sequence:

🎯 Goal Formation

Turning the problem into a goal expressed in positive, concrete language.

✨ The Miracle Question

Describing the desired future in vivid, behavioral detail.

🔍 Exceptions and Strengths

Uncovering already-existing resources and successful moments.

📏 Scaling and Small Steps

Measuring progress and moving forward with accessible, concrete steps.

💡 Important note: SFBT's brief nature (usually 5-10 sessions) sets it apart from other models — the goal isn't deep analysis, but rapid, sustained progress. When I first applied this model myself, the hardest part was building the habit of 'not asking about the problem' — it took time to unlearn the classic interview reflex trained over years, but the results are worth it.

Clinical Case: Roya's Story

Roya was 10 years old; her homeroom teacher called her parents to say that in recent months she'd been having frequent, small arguments with friends, and would explode over small things. Her mother wanted to fully explain "her problem" at the first meeting, but when I gave Roya the floor herself, I saw something different — she was tired of being known as "the angry girl," and wanted to be known differently. That's the strength of SFBT: instead of digging into the history of "what's going wrong," we searched together for the answer to "how does she want to be."

SectionNotes (Roya's example)
Presenting problemFrequent anger, conflict with friends, excessive reactions to small incidents.
Precipitating factorsHer closest friend moving to a different class, a new sibling in the family drawing attention.
Predisposing factorsHigh emotional sensitivity, limited modeling of "expressing feelings" in the family.
Perpetuating factorsThe "angry girl" label, others around her seeing her as problematic, and its effect on her self-perception.
Protective factorsCreative imagination, enthusiasm for drawing, a trusting relationship with her mother.
Core belief"I'll always be angry, I can't change" — a fixed, unchangeable self-schema.
Treatment goalShifting from the "Problem Roya" story to the "Calm Me" story, making the already-existing exception moments visible.

SpeakerLine
TherapistRoya, imagine that tonight while you sleep a miracle happens, and the "angry girl" story completely changes. What's the first thing you'd notice when you wake up tomorrow?
RoyaI don't know... maybe I'd feel lighter.
TherapistWhat would feeling light look like from the outside?
RoyaI'd smile, I'd talk without rushing.

📝 Homework example (for Roya): This week, note down in a small notebook every moment the 'Calm Me' state occurs (even if it's just 2 minutes) — the goal isn't the outcome, just seeing that these moments already exist. The parent is also advised to listen to these notes with curiosity, without criticism, since every observation is itself already progress.

Assessment Tool: ORS (Outcome Rating Scale)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Duncan and Miller (2000), an ultra-brief, client-rated outcome measure across 4 visual lines (personal, relational, social, overall) — fully aligned with SFBT's resource-focused philosophy.

🎯 Why it's given

It takes less than 1 minute at the start of each session, tracking how the client feels in their own words and comparing progress from session to session.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist or any therapist trained in SFBT; the child version (Smiley Faces ORS) can be used from age 6.

🧰 Materials used

Standard ORS sheet (4 lines), a ruler or millimeter measure, a chart for graphing session-to-session results.

Recommendations for Parents

🔍 Ask "what worked?"

Focus not on the details of the bad day, but on what made the good moments different.

📏 Praise small progress

Even moving up 1 point on the scale is a big success — take it seriously and note it.

💬 Use positive language

Speak in the form of "what you want to do," instead of "what you shouldn't do."

10

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Situations requiring special attention in the stuttering program

Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) is a powerful, effective method, but during an active crisis, stabilization is needed first, before 'future-oriented' questions:

  • The client showing active thoughts of harming themselves or others;
  • Observation of psychotic symptoms (perceptions not grounded in reality, disorganized speech);
  • Ongoing, current exposure to violence (at home, at school).
  1. When these signs are detected, immediately stop the "miracle question" and other future-oriented techniques.
  2. Ask direct, concrete risk questions: "Have you thought about hurting yourself? When, how?"
  3. If risk is confirmed, apply safety planning (the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning model) and inform the parent.
  4. After stabilization, once the clinical situation allows, you can return to SFBT — but this is not automatic, it is a clinical decision.

📌 Topic Summary

  • SFBT is a brief model that focuses not on the details of the problem, but on the desired future and existing resources.
  • Core techniques: the Miracle Question, Exception Finding, Scaling Questions, and the strengths-based approach.
  • Small, concrete steps make big changes accessible and sustain ongoing motivation.
  • The model, especially with its brief (8-session) format, produces fast, positive results with children and adolescents.
