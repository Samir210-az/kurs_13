Topic 07 · Play Therapy

📖 Play and Fairy Tale Therapy

An 8-session program — a therapeutic model that creates safe access to a child's inner world through story metaphors.

1

Scientific Definition: The Basics of Fairy Tale Therapy

Clinical definition: Fairy Tale Therapy (Gilligan & Price, 1993; Bettelheim's "The Uses of Enchantment", 1976) is based on the therapeutic power of metaphor — instead of expressing their emotional experience directly, the child can work "from a safe distance" through story characters. This overlaps with narrative therapy's principle of "externalization" (White & Epston, 1990) — the problem is presented not as part of the child, but as separate, as an image like a 'monster,' so the child can actively fight against it.
In simple terms: Telling a child directly, "Talk about your fear," often doesn't work — the child shuts down. But if we ask, "If this fear were a monster, what would it look like?", the child suddenly begins to speak freely. A fairy tale is like a 'changing room' for emotions — the feeling is expressed not directly, but 'dressed up' through a character.

This program is especially effective for working with fear, social difficulty, and problems with emotional expression in young children (ages 4-10). What surprised me most the first time I applied this method was the precision in children's story choices — no one picks a book by accident. The therapist's job is to 'read' this choice correctly and to hear, through the character, what the child isn't saying in their own words.

2

Areas of Application for Fairy Tale Therapy

The areas below are the contexts where the method is most often used, but with a creative approach it can be adapted to almost any emotional difficulty.

😨

Fears

Darkness, loneliness, specific phobias — externalized through the 'monster' metaphor.

E.g.: The story of the 'Dark Monster.'

🤝

Social Difficulties

Friendship problems, disputes — solutions are found through animal characters.

E.g.: the rabbit-and-turtle story.

💭

Emotional Expression

Difficulty expressing feelings directly — indirect expression through puppets and characters.

E.g.: 'talking' through a puppet.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

Metaphoric distance allows the child to approach their own emotional material without fear of being judged, which speeds up the therapeutic process.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Puppet and drawing activities support fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination while carrying therapeutic content at the same time.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

Soft materials (puppets, fabrics) create a calming effect through touch, making the storytelling process more comfortable.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The storytelling process develops speech structure (beginning-middle-end), vocabulary, and narrative skills.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

The family story ritual supports the development of structured narrative skills that can also be applied at school.

4

Age Groups

The choice of story and the complexity of language change significantly by age — concrete, familiar stories work with younger children, while complex, multi-layered plots are more effective with older children.

👶 Early age (4-6 years)

  • Simple, familiar stories (Little Red Riding Hood, etc.) are used as the main material
  • Puppets and soft toys are especially effective
  • Parental participation makes the process easier

📚 Middle age (7-9 years)

  • The child is more capable of creating their own story independently
  • Written elements (creating a book) can be added
  • More complex metaphors (interplanetary travel) can be used

🎓 Older age (10+ years)

  • The method can be applied to more abstract emotional topics (identity, change)
  • The child can interpret the symbolic meaning of the metaphor themselves
  • A written story format (in a diary/journal form) may be preferred
5

Core Model: The Therapeutic Journey of the Story

The program takes the child through a 4-stage 'story journey':

📚 Choice and Identification

Discovering which story/character the child identifies with.

🎭 Metaphoric Expression

Indirect expression of feelings through puppets and characters.

🐉 Externalization

Turning the fear/problem into a separate 'monster' image and fighting it.

✍️ Creative Ownership

The child creates their own story, which becomes a family ritual.

Clinical Case: Kamila's Story

Kamila was 6 years old when her kindergarten teacher referred her, unable to find an exact way to describe it — "she's just very quiet, she almost never talks to anyone." Her mother added that she was talkative at home, but went completely silent with new people. In my first play session with Kamila, I didn't ask for words — I simply placed storybooks in front of her and waited to see which one she'd reach for. This is exactly the power of fairy tale therapy: sometimes a child says a great deal without speaking. In such cases, asking a direct question, like "why don't you talk?", usually backfires — the child withdraws even more. But a non-threatening activity like choosing among storybooks gives the child a sense of control, and it's precisely this feeling that makes the first connection possible.

Assessment Test: House-Tree-Person Test

ℹ️ About the test

A classic projective drawing test developed by Buck (1948) — the child draws a house, a tree, and a person; the elements of the drawing (size, placement, details) symbolically reflect the emotional state.

🎯 Why it's given

To explore, in a non-verbal way, the inner emotional landscape of children who have difficulty expressing themselves in words (like Kamila).

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist trained in projective testing; interpretation must always be combined with clinical observation and is not diagnostic on its own.

🧰 Materials used

A blank sheet of paper, an ordinary pencil (colored pencils as an additional option), an interpretation guide.

As in Kamila's example, projective tests should never be treated as 'proof' on their own — they are simply a window that supports clinical observation and should be read together with other sources of information, not in isolation.

Recommendations for Parents

The effect of fairy tale therapy grows even stronger when it extends beyond the session room. My work with Kamila's family showed that a small ritual continued at home can have far more impact than 1 hour at the clinic.

📖 Create a regular story ritual

Reading/creating a story at a fixed time each evening strengthens your bond with your child.

🎭 Take the metaphor seriously

When your child talks about a character, this is often their own feelings — listen without judging.

✍️ Encourage creativity

Let your child create their own stories, and let them know there's no such thing as 'right' or 'wrong.'

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Disclosure of trauma during symbolic play

In fairy tale and play therapy, children often reveal trauma material not through direct words, but through symbolic storytelling. This requires special sensitivity from the therapist:

  • Repeated depiction, through story characters, of violence/abuse scenes that closely resemble real events;
  • The child suddenly freezing during play, a dissociative appearance (blank stare, loss of contact);
  • Disclosures that start 'in the name of the character' and then shift to the pronoun 'I.'
  1. Follow the symbolic material without changing it or bombarding the child with questions — just support with "go on, I'm here"; questioning can distort trauma details.
  2. When an open, direct disclosure occurs (outside the character, using 'I' language), record it calmly, without altering the child's own words.
  3. Begin the legally mandated reporting procedure immediately after the session.
  4. During signs of dissociation, switch to grounding techniques — restoring sensory connection, such as naming 5 objects seen or 4 sounds heard.
  5. Consult with the child protection agency before informing the parent (in cases where the parent's safety cannot be assumed).

📌 Topic Summary

  • Fairy tale therapy creates safe access to a child's emotional material through metaphoric distance.
  • Externalization (turning fear into a 'monster' image) allows the child to actively fight their problem.
  • The child creating their own story is the most powerful 'ownership' stage of the therapeutic process.
  • The family story ritual transfers the therapeutic effect home and strengthens long-term attachment.
