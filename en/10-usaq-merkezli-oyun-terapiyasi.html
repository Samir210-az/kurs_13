Topic 10 · Play Therapy

🧸 Child-Centered Play Therapy

An 8-session program — a non-directive model based on Virginia Axline's principles, oriented toward the child's development at their own pace.

1

Scientific Definition: Foundations of Child-Centered Play Therapy

Clinical definition: Child-Centered Play Therapy (Virginia Axline, 1947) is the application of Carl Rogers's humanistic, client-centered therapy principles to children. At the core of the model lies a deep belief in the child's innate capacity for self-actualization. Axline's 8 classic principles (genuine rapport, full acceptance, permissiveness, reflecting feelings, respect for the child's own problems, non-directiveness, not rushing, only necessary limits) define the therapist's role not as an "active healer," but as one who creates a safe, accepting environment.
In simple terms: Imagine planting a seed — you don't grow it, you just provide the right soil, water, and light, and the seed grows itself. Child-Centered Play Therapy is the same — the therapist doesn't tell the child what to do, just creates a safe, accepting 'soil' so the child can grow themselves with their own inner resources.

Unlike other structured models, this model is non-directive — it doesn't teach a specific technique, instead it trusts in the healing power of the therapeutic relationship itself. In practice, this is one of the hardest skills for a therapist — letting go of the instinct to 'fix,' not filling silence, allowing the child to go at their own pace. In my experience, the results of this patience often become visible as the sessions progress, not in the first session — the child gradually internalizes the feeling of "I'm not being judged here."

Axline's 8 Principles (briefly)

These 8 principles aren't separate from each other — in a real session, all of them are applied simultaneously, interwoven.

🤗

Genuine Rapport

The therapist builds a warm, genuine connection with the child, as quickly as possible.

The foundation of the trust relationship.

✅

Full Acceptance

The child is accepted as they are, no change is required.

Lays the foundation for self-acceptance.

🪞

Reflecting Feelings

The therapist reflects the child's feelings with such sensitivity that the child understands themselves better.

The role of a mirror.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

The non-directive approach allows the child to process emotions at their own pace, which is especially important in cases of trauma and loss.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

A structured, limited selection of toys (Axline's recommendation) allows deep engagement without distracting the child's attention.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

The child engaging with sensory activities of their own choosing (colored paints, tactile materials) naturally supports self-regulation.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

The therapist's precise, simple reflective language serves as a model for expanding the child's emotional vocabulary.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

The principle of "listening without judgment" learned from this model can also be applied in teacher-child relationships.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early age (3-6 years)

  • The model was originally developed for this age group
  • Symbolic play (puppets) is the main means of expression
  • Universal applicability, not dependent on speech development

📚 Middle age (7-10 years)

  • Drawing and creative activities serve as an additional means of expression
  • The child shows their own problems more independently in symbolic play
  • Verbal expression also increases in parallel with symbolic play

🎓 Older age (11+ years)

  • The model can be adapted to be less toy-oriented, more conversation-oriented
  • The core principles (acceptance, reflecting feelings) remain unchanged
  • Alternative means of expression like creative writing and music can be added
5

Core Model: Axline's 8 Principles

The program emphasizes Axline's classic 8 principles across consecutive sessions:

1-2: Rapport and Acceptance

Genuine connection and full, non-judgmental acceptance.

3-4: Freedom and Reflection

A permissive environment and precise reflection of feelings.

5-6: Respect and Pace Matching

Respect for the child's own problem-solving ability and not rushing.

7-8: Non-directiveness and Limits

Avoiding direction, only necessary limits.

Clinical Case: Vusal's Story

Vusal was 6 years old, referred by his parents who said, "he's very sensitive, he cries over small things." In the first session I didn't give him any task — I just showed him the toys in the room and said, "you can do what you want here." This is exactly Axline's philosophy: the child has the capacity to heal themselves, the therapist's job isn't to 'fix' this, but to create a safe space for it. In the early sessions Vusal would often look at me and ask with his eyes, "is it allowed?" — this showed how much he'd been taught outside to behave 'correctly.' Over time this waiting decreased, he began deciding for himself — this is actually one of the program's key indicators, since independent decision-making is a cornerstone of self-regulation and lays the foundation for future emotional maturity and healthy decision-making habits.

📝 Homework example (for Vusal's family): This model doesn't require classic 'homework' — instead, the parent is recommended to also reflect Vusal's feelings at home without correcting them, simply naming them (like "you're sad right now"). Over time, this small language shift gradually leads the child to recognize their own feelings more comfortably, name them in their own words, and express them calmly.

Assessment Test: Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Achenbach, a standard tool filled out by parent/teacher, measuring a broad behavioral and emotional profile.

🎯 Why it's given

To document the child's overall emotional-behavioral picture before starting the program, to objectively track progress.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist; the parent and teacher forms are filled out in parallel and compared.

🧰 Materials used

A standard CBCL sheet, a scoring key, a profile chart.

As in Vusal's example, in the non-directive model this assessment isn't the only tool for "measuring progress" — the therapist's session notes are at least as valuable.

Recommendations for Parents

The non-directive approach can't be fully applied at home — the parent has their own daily responsibilities. But the small adjustments below make a big difference for children like Vusal.

👂 Listen without judging

When your child shares something, instead of immediately offering a solution, first reflect their feeling: "You're sad about that."

⏳ Respect their own pace

Children go through emotional processes at different speeds — support them patiently instead of rushing them.

🎨 Take play seriously

Your child's play isn't 'just play,' it's an expression of their inner world — show interest, don't judge.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Trauma re-enactment in non-directive play

Because the therapist doesn't direct the play in the child-centered, non-directive format, trauma re-enactment can surface more freely:

  • The same traumatic scene (being hit, screaming, hiding) being repeated unchanged across sessions ("post-traumatic play");
  • A sudden dissociative episode during play — freezing of movement, blank eyes;
  • Unusually strong aggression toward toys, reflecting a real scene of violence.
  1. When you recognize a pattern of post-traumatic play, continue observing without violating the principle (non-directiveness), but keep careful clinical notes.
  2. If dissociation occurs, call the child by name, bring them back to the present moment calmly saying "you're here, with me."
  3. When repeated scenes reflecting real violence are detected, contact the parent (if they're safe) and begin the reporting procedure if necessary.
  4. If the post-traumatic play still hasn't changed after 6-8 sessions (no sign of relief or integration), make a parallel referral to a trauma-focused therapist.

📌 Topic Summary

  • Child-Centered Play Therapy is based on Rogers's humanistic principles — a deep belief in the child's capacity for self-healing.
  • Axline's 8 principles (acceptance, permissiveness, reflecting feelings, respect, pace matching, minimal limits) define the therapist's non-directive role.
  • The model doesn't teach a specific technique — the therapeutic relationship itself is the source of change.
  • Respect for the child's ability to solve their own problems at their own pace lays the foundation for self-confidence and independence.
