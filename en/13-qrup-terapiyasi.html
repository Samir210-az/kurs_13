Topic 13 · Group Model

👥 Group Therapy Program for Children and Teenagers

An 8-session program — a group model that strengthens social-emotional skills through mutual experience with peers.

1

Scientific Definition: Foundations of Group Therapy

Clinical definition: Unlike individual therapy, Group Therapy (Irvin Yalom, "The Theory and Practice of Group Psychotherapy," 1970) is based on unique mechanisms called "therapeutic factors": universality (seeing that your own problem isn't unique), group cohesion (a sense of belonging), interpersonal learning (real-time social skill practice), and instillation of hope (seeing others' progress). Tuckman's (1965) group development model (forming-storming-norming-performing) explains the group's staged development — deep work isn't possible without initial safety being established.
In simple terms: In individual therapy, the therapist is a 'mirror' — reflecting back what you say. In group therapy, there are several 'mirrors' — each peer shows you a different perspective, shares their own experience. This creates a kind of learning that isn't possible alone: 'I'm not alone, others have experienced this too.'

This program offers unique social learning opportunities that can't be achieved in individual sessions, especially in the areas of social skill development and self-esteem. Running group therapy requires a completely different skill set than individual sessions — the therapist must track both each individual member and the whole group dynamic at the same time. In my experience, the most sensitive moment is the 'storming' stage, because many therapists try to suppress conflict, when in fact it's a natural, necessary part of the group's healthy development.

2

Yalom's Core Therapeutic Factors

Yalom identified 11 therapeutic factors; the 3 that work most powerfully in teen groups are presented below.

🌍

Universality

Seeing that your own problem/feeling isn't unique, that others experience it too.

Reduces the feeling of isolation.

🤝

Group Cohesion

The sense of belonging to and being accepted by the group.

The foundation of psychological safety.

🔄

Interpersonal Learning

Real-time social skill practice with real people.

An advantage not possible in individual therapy.

3

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological Perspective

Group dynamics enrich children's social cognition and empathy in real time, with multiple perspectives.

✋ Occupational Therapist's Perspective

Team activities (like tower building) develop both motor coordination and social cooperation in parallel.

🌊 Sensory Integration Perspective

Managing the sensory load of a group setting (noise, movement) may require individual support, especially for sensory-sensitive children.

🗣️ Speech and Language Therapist's Perspective

Turn-taking, active listening, and self-expression within the group create the most effective practice environment for pragmatic language skills.

🏫 Psychopedagogical Perspective

The cooperation and conflict resolution skills learned in the group can transfer directly to the classroom setting.

4

Age Groups

👶 Early age (5-7 years)

  • Short, play-based activities predominate
  • Group size is kept small (3-4 people)
  • Visual support (cards, pictures) is used often

📚 Middle age (8-12 years)

  • Structured activities and free discussion are balanced
  • Group size can expand up to 4-6 people
  • Social skills training takes a more central role

🎓 Adolescence (13+ years)

  • A more conversation-based, less play-based format
  • Topics like identity, friendship, and the future become central
  • Peer feedback carries especially strong influence
5

Core Model: Tuckman's Stages of Group Development

The program follows Tuckman's (1965) classic group development model:

1️⃣ Forming

Acquaintance, forming rules, initial safety.

2️⃣ Storming

Conflicts arising and being resolved healthily (a natural stage).

3️⃣ Norming

Deepening trust, strengthening cooperation.

4️⃣ Performing/Adjourning

Full functioning and healthy closure (termination).

Clinical Case: The Story of a Group of 5

The group consisted of 5 teenagers — Aygun, Rashad, Nargiz, Kamran, and Ayan, all ages 13-14, all from different classes and coming with different problems: some with social anxiety, some with aggression management. In the first session there was heavy silence in the room — no one wanted to take the first step. As Yalom said, the power of group therapy lies exactly in this initial anxiety turning into a feeling of 'universality': the discovery of 'I'm not alone.' When forming the group, I made careful choices — the goal wasn't to bring together children with very similar problems, but a diversity that could complement each other, because different perspectives enrich the group's learning. An initial fit interview was conducted individually with each candidate, and expectations were also explained clearly and in advance to the families.

📝 Homework example (for the group): Each member tries 1 skill they learned in the group this week (for example, active listening) with a friend in real life, and briefly shares it at the next session. This sharing further strengthens group cohesion.

Assessment Test: Group Therapy Usefulness Questionnaire (GTQ)

ℹ️ About the test

A short questionnaire based on Yalom's therapeutic factors model, measuring which factors (universality, hope, belonging, etc.) group members find most useful.

🎯 Why it's given

To determine which therapeutic factors the group is working strongest on, and shape future sessions accordingly.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist trained in group therapy; usually applied in the middle and at the end of the program.

🧰 Materials used

An anonymous question sheet for each member, a chart for summarizing results at the group level.

As in the example of the group of 5, the anonymous survey format gives members the necessary sense of safety to share their real opinions — thoughts often unheard in open discussion come to the surface.

Recommendations for Parents

The parent's role in group therapy is different — due to the confidentiality principle, you don't have full right to know what happens within the group, but you can create a supportive environment at home.

🔒 Support confidentiality

Avoid asking your child who said what in the group — instead ask "What did you share yourself?"

📅 Ensure consistency

Consistent participation in group therapy is critical for building safety and trust — ensure attendance at every session possible.

🎉 Take closure seriously

When the program ends, approach your child's feelings (sadness, gratitude) with respect — this is a healthy way of learning to say goodbye.

10

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Crisis within the group and the "contagion effect"

Group therapy has its own unique risk — one member's crisis can affect the others too (especially the "contagion effect" on topics of self-harm/suicide):

  • A member disclosing thoughts of self-harm/suicide within the group;
  • The risk of similar thoughts being awakened in other members after this disclosure;
  • One member being systematically isolated/bullied within the group dynamic.
  1. During a risk disclosure, calmly bring the group to a pause without embarrassing them (if there's no co-therapist, switch to a brief structured activity).
  2. Conduct a structured risk assessment of the C-SSRS type with the disclosing member separately, in an individual room.
  3. Create an opportunity for the remaining group members to process their feelings to reduce the "contagion effect," but without repeating the details.
  4. Inform each participant's parent in an appropriate way — prioritize safety while also protecting confidentiality.
  5. If bullying is detected within the group, immediately restore clear group rules, temporarily switching to individual format if needed.

📌 Topic Summary

  • Group Therapy is based on Yalom's therapeutic factors (universality, group cohesion, interpersonal learning).
  • Tuckman's 4-stage model (forming-storming-norming-performing) explains the group's natural development path.
  • Real-time social skill practice with multiple peers is a unique learning experience not achievable in individual therapy.
  • A healthy closure (termination) process offers children a valuable model for future partings.
