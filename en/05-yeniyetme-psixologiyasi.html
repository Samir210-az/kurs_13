Topic 05 · The Adolescent Period

🎓 Adolescent Psychology

A 12-session program across 4 sub-topics: Self-Esteem, Social Withdrawal, Exam Stress, and Socialization — the core psychological challenges of adolescence.

1

Scientific Background: The Psychological Challenges of Adolescence

Clinical definition: According to Erikson's theory of psychosocial development (1968), adolescence is the "Identity vs. Role Confusion" stage — where self-esteem, social belonging, and the search for independence take center stage. During this period, the prefrontal cortex is not yet fully mature (Steinberg, 2008), which can heighten difficulties with emotional regulation, social anxiety, and academic stress. This program offers structured, evidence-based interventions across 4 core areas — self-esteem, social withdrawal, exam stress, and socialization.
In simple terms: Adolescence is the period when the question "Who am I?" is asked most intensely. The body is changing, the brain hasn't fully "matured" yet, and the social world suddenly becomes far more complex. This program offers the teenager concrete tools for finding themselves within this storm of change.

Each of the 4 sub-topics is built as a separate, yet complementary block, and can be applied in any order or independently. What I often see in practice is that these 4 areas actually reinforce one another — a teenager with solid self-esteem also becomes more resilient to exam stress, while one who develops social skills moves further away from the extra anxiety that stems from both withdrawal and isolation. That's why I recommend presenting these blocks not as separate "problems," but as stages of a single developmental journey.

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Views)

🧠 Psychological perspective

A combination of cognitive-behavioral therapy (Beck), SFBT, and social skills training supports the teenager's parallel development across all 4 areas.

✋ Occupational therapist perspective

Relaxation techniques (progressive muscle relaxation) can be applied in both the context of exam stress and social anxiety.

🌊 Sensory Integration perspective

High stress levels increase the nervous system's state of arousal — calming sensory strategies are useful across all 4 sub-topics.

🗣️ Speech-language therapist perspective

Assertive communication and active listening skills are directly connected to the development of pragmatic language skills.

🏫 Psychopedagogical perspective

Study strategies and time management support academic performance, creating additional opportunities for application in the school setting.

4

Age Groups

Adolescence itself spans a wide range — the needs, language level, and desire for autonomy of a 12-year-old and a 17-year-old are very different, so the program should be adjusted according to age group.

👶 Early adolescence (ages 11-13)

  • Topics of self-esteem and social belonging are especially relevant
  • Concrete, visual materials (journals, cards) are more effective
  • Parental support still plays an important role at this age

📚 Middle adolescence (ages 14-16)

  • Exam stress and academic pressure increase
  • Peer relationships take on central importance
  • The need for independent thought and self-expression grows stronger

🎓 Late adolescence (ages 17+)

  • Identity formation is in its finalizing stage
  • Decisions about the future (university, career) can create stress
  • Assertiveness and independent problem-solving skills take priority

Core Model: 4 Parallel Blocks

Each sub-program is grounded in its own distinct scientific basis:

💪 Self-Esteem — Bandura & Beck

Self-efficacy theory and cognitive restructuring.

🚪 Social Withdrawal — Wolpe

Systematic desensitization and graded exposure.

📝 Exam Stress — Sarason

The cognitive-physiological model of test anxiety.

🤝 Socialization — Rogers & Alberti

Active listening and assertive communication.

Clinical Case: Aysel's Story

Aysel was 15 years old when her homeroom teacher referred her — the reason was mixed: she barely spoke in class, and during the last exam week she had left the classroom in tears twice. Her mother said at the first meeting, "she wasn't like this before." But in conversation with Aysel herself, something different became clear to me — the problem wasn't "shyness," it was the harsh, critical inner voice she had built about herself. This is actually a very familiar picture — during the transition from middle school to high school, many teenagers go through the same leap: a child who was comfortable in a smaller school suddenly finds herself in a larger, more competitive environment, and her old self-perception no longer works.

Assessment Test: Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Rosenberg (1965), this is the most widely used 10-item self-esteem measure in the world. It is answered using a 4-point Likert scale.

🎯 Why it's administered

To measure the baseline level of self-esteem at the start of the program, and to determine which of the 4 sub-blocks needs more focus.

🎓 Who can administer it

Clinical psychologist; used directly as a self-report form with teenagers, with widely validated translation reliability.

🧰 Materials used

Standard 10-item question sheet, scoring key (range of 10-40 points), table for recording results.

As with Aysel's example, the result of this scale does not by itself provide a diagnosis — it only offers a starting point for conversation. The real value lies not in the number itself, but in hearing what the teenager thinks about that number.

Recommendations for Parents

Working with a teenager is a team effort — progress made in the session room often fades quickly if it isn't continued at home. The recommendations below are distilled from my experience working with the parents of clients like Aysel.

👂 Listen without judgment

When the teenager shares their difficulties, listen first without immediately offering a solution, and let them feel heard.

🎯 Recognize small progress

Without expecting a big change, praise every small courageous step (a new acquaintance, expressing an opinion).

🕰️ Allow autonomy

Give the teenager room to make their own decisions — this strengthens their sense of independence and self-esteem.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Suicide and self-harm risk in adolescents — a comprehensive protocol

When working with the adolescent age group, the therapist must remain sensitive to covert risk signals in every session:

  • Direct statements: "I'm tired," "it would be easier for everyone," "I want to leave";
  • Giving away belongings, farewell-like posts on social media;
  • Sharp changes in sleep/appetite, complete withdrawal from previous interests;
  • Signs of self-harm (on the arms, thighs) — concealed by a change in clothing;
  • The onset or increase of substance use.
  1. When risk is suspected, apply a structured sequence of questions in line with the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS): ideation → plan → intent → past attempt.
  2. If active intent/plan is identified, immediately involve the parent in the session without leaving the teenager alone.
  3. Prepare a concrete plan in line with the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning model: warning signs, coping strategies, support network, restricting access to means, emergency contact numbers.
  4. In high-risk cases, make an immediate urgent referral to child psychiatry, and document this decision together with the parent.
  5. In low-to-moderate risk cases, set up an accelerated follow-up schedule and reassess risk at every session.

📌 Topic Summary

  • The adolescent period is characterized by Erikson's "Identity vs. Role Confusion" stage and creates a need for support across 4 core areas (self-esteem, socialization, stress, social anxiety).
  • Each sub-program is based on a distinct yet complementary scientific model (Bandura, Wolpe, Sarason, Rogers).
  • Small, concrete steps and regular self-assessment ensure continuous progress across all 4 areas.
  • Parental support is critically important during this period — in a balanced way, through both listening and allowing autonomy.
