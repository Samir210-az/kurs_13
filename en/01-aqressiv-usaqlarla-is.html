Topic 01 · Behavior Management

🔥 Working with Aggressive Children and Adolescents Program

A 16-session structured program based on the Aggression Replacement Training (ART) model — a complete path from the roots of aggression to genuine self-management skills.

1

Scientific Definition: The Clinical Basis of Aggression

Clinical definition: Aggression is behavior intended to cause physical, verbal, or psychological harm to another person. According to the DSM-5-TR, repeated, developmentally inappropriate aggression can meet the diagnostic criteria for Oppositional Defiant Disorder, Conduct Disorder, or Intermittent Explosive Disorder. Dodge's Social Information Processing Model (1994) shows that aggressive children tend to interpret neutral social cues as "hostile intent" (hostile attribution bias). Two main forms of aggression are distinguished: reactive aggression (impulsive, tied to emotional arousal, resulting from amygdala hyperreactivity and weak prefrontal regulation) and proactive aggression (planned, used instrumentally, learned through modeling according to Bandura's Social Learning Theory).
In simple terms: An aggressive child's brain can be compared to a house with an oversensitive alarm system — even a small noise (like a dog barking) sets it off as though a burglar has broken in. When the body receives a "danger" signal, it reacts with "fight" without thinking. Our goal is to make this alarm system "less sensitive" and to teach the child to choose a different reaction when the signal goes off.

Aggression isn't about being a "bad child." It is often the result of a learnable skill gap (impulse control, emotional regulation, social problem-solving), and it can be significantly reduced with proper, structured intervention.

2

Clinical Classification of Aggression

⚡

Reactive Aggression

An impulsive, unplanned response to emotional arousal (e.g. feeling insulted or threatened).

E.g.: pushing someone back immediately after being pushed.

🎯

Proactive Aggression

Planned aggression used as a "tool" to achieve a specific goal (control, status, obtaining an object).

E.g.: deliberately pushing someone else to get a toy.

🗣️

Relational/Verbal Aggression

Causing harm without physical contact — through mockery, insults, exclusion, or spreading rumors.

E.g.: deliberately excluding a peer from the group.

3

Multidisciplinary View (5 Specialist Perspectives)

🧠 Psychological perspective

Aggression is often comorbid with ADHD, a trauma history, or family conflict — these factors must always be explored during assessment. Standardized tools like the Buss-Perry Aggression Questionnaire provide an objective way to measure this.

✋ Occupational therapist's perspective

Healthy release of pent-up physical energy (proprioceptive activities — jumping, squeezing, pushing games) can reduce the frequency of aggressive episodes, especially in sensory-seeking children.

🌊 Sensory integration perspective

In some children, aggression is actually a "fight-or-flight" response from a dysregulated nervous system — the body is constantly in "danger" mode, and calming strategies help regulate the nervous system.

🗣️ Speech therapist's perspective

Weak pragmatic language skills (expressing feelings in words, taking turns in conversation) often turn frustration into aggression — "a child without words speaks with their hands."

🏫 Psycho-pedagogical perspective

The structure of the classroom environment (clear rules, transitions announced in advance, an accessible de-escalation space) directly affects the frequency of aggressive episodes.

4

Presentation by Age Group

👶 Preschool (ages 4-6)

  • Physical aggression (hitting, biting, pushing) is more common
  • Limited vocabulary means frustration translates directly into action
  • Parent training (Parent Management Training) is the priority intervention

📚 Primary school (ages 7-11)

  • Aggression takes both physical and verbal forms — fighting, insults
  • The risk of peer rejection increases
  • Social skills training (Skillstreaming) is especially effective

🎓 Adolescence (age 12+)

  • Aggression is more often verbal, relational, and sometimes directed at property
  • Peer pressure and group dynamics play a significant role
  • Focus shifts toward self-regulation and moral reasoning skills
5

Core Model: Aggression Replacement Training (ART)

The ART model, developed by Goldstein, Glick, and Gibbs (1998), is based on replacing aggression not with a single method but with 3 parallel components. This program integrates these 3 components across 16 sessions:

💪 Skillstreaming (Social Skills Training)

Teaching concrete social skills (responding to criticism, withstanding peer pressure) step by step through modeling and role-play.

🌡️ Anger Control Training

Recognizing triggers, reading physiological signals (Anger Thermometer), and the Stop-Think-Act technique.

⚖️ Moral Reasoning

Strengthening empathy and a sense of justice through discussion of ethical dilemmas (aligned with Kohlberg's stages of moral development).

👨‍👩‍👧 Family Integration

A Parent Management Training component — building a consistent support system at home.

💡 Important note: Research shows that children taught only anger control retain a high risk of relapse — the strength of ART lies precisely in applying these 3 components TOGETHER.

Clinical Case: Kenan's Story

Kenan was 9 years old when the school administration referred him to us, and the reason was clear enough — over the past two months, he had been involved in 6 physical altercations during recess. At the first meeting, his mother said he often fought with his sister at home too, just not as intensely. What struck me most when I first met him, honestly, wasn't the aggression — it was the fear in his eyes. As I've often seen, anger here too was covering something else: a fear of feeling belittled.

Assessment tool: Buss-Perry Aggression Questionnaire

ℹ️ About the tool

A 29-item self-report scale developed by Buss and Perry in 1992. It is divided into 4 subscales: Physical Aggression, Verbal Aggression, Anger, and Hostility.

🎯 Why it's used

To objectively measure which form of aggression (physical/verbal) and which component (impulse or hostile attribution) is stronger, and to shape the intervention plan accordingly.

🎓 Who can administer it

A clinical psychologist or a certified specialist in child and adolescent care; completed via self-report with adolescents, and with a parent/teacher CBCL form for younger children.

🧰 Materials used

A printed question sheet, a pen, a quiet room; a scoring table for recording results (range 29-145, calculated separately by subscale).

Tips for Parents

🌡️ Use thermometer language

When your child is getting tense, instead of "Calm down!" ask "Where are you on the thermometer?" — this directs them toward self-assessment without judgment.

🚫 Don't lecture during an aggressive moment

The capacity to hear explanations/reprimands drops to a minimum during high emotional arousal — help them calm down first, save the conversation for later.

🎯 Praise the effort, not just the outcome

"This time you stopped and thought — that's a big step" is far more effective praise than the abstract "you're a good kid."

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ When immediate intervention is needed

Within the aggression program, when the following signs appear, the session plan is set aside and safety becomes the priority:

  • Access to a weapon (knife, tool, harmful object at home) or a direct threat in this direction;
  • A threat with a specific target — the presence of the "plan + means + time" triad;
  • Episodes of cruelty to animals — can be an indicator of escalating behavior;
  • Witnessing or showing signs of being exposed to physical violence at home;
  • Aggression turning inward toward the self ("I don't want to live" statements, signs of self-harm).
  1. Ask directly, in a calm, non-judgmental tone: "Have you had thoughts of hurting someone else, or yourself?" — a direct question does not increase risk, it increases clarity.
  2. If the danger seems real and imminent, notify the parent/guardian immediately; do not leave the child alone.
  3. If the plan+means+time triad is present, refer to the nearest child psychiatric emergency service, coordinating this step together with the parent.
  4. Discuss concretely with the parent how to restrict access to weapons and sharp objects at home (means restriction).
  5. Document every crisis episode with the date, the risk factors identified, and the steps taken.
  6. If suspected abuse against the child arises, do not forget the duty to report to the relevant child protection authority.

Note: If Conduct Disorder symptoms are found to be comorbid, referral for a broader psychiatric assessment is mandatory — the ART model alone may not be sufficient.

📌 Topic summary

  • Aggression presents in reactive (impulsive-emotional) and proactive (planned-instrumental) forms, each requiring a different intervention.
  • The ART model consists of 3 parallel components: Skillstreaming (social skills), Anger Control Training, and Moral Reasoning.
  • The Anger Thermometer and the Stop-Think-Act technique teach the child to recognize physiological signals early and create a pause between impulse and action.
  • Parent involvement (Parent Management Training) significantly increases the long-term effectiveness of the intervention.
