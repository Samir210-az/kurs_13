Scientific Definition: The Clinical Basis of Stuttering

Clinical definition: Stuttering (Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder in the DSM-5-TR) is a speech disorder that disrupts the normal rhythm of speech through sound/syllable repetitions, sound prolongations, and blocks (complete stoppage of airflow/voice) that are atypical in frequency and duration for the child's age. These are called "core behaviors." Over time, children may develop "secondary behaviors" in reaction to stuttering — eye blinking, facial tension, word substitution, avoidance. Most stuttering is developmental in origin (emerging between ages 2-6, linked to genetic predisposition), and rarely neurogenic (linked to brain injury).
In simple terms: Speaking is a complex act that requires the brain to act as a "conductor," coordinating the lips, tongue, vocal folds, and lungs all at once. During stuttering, this "orchestra" sometimes loses its synchronization — as if several musicians suddenly started playing at different tempos. Our goal is to resynchronize this "orchestra."

Stuttering does not mean a child is speaking "wrong" — it's a temporary difficulty in the motor planning of speech, and it can improve significantly with proper, structured speech-language intervention.

2

Clinical Classification of Stuttering

🔁

Repetitions

Repeating a sound, syllable, or word several times.

E.g.: "I-I-I am going".

➰

Prolongations

Unusually stretching out a sound, where airflow continues but the sound doesn't change.

E.g.: "Iiiiii am going".

⛔

Blocks

Complete stoppage of airflow or voice, a feeling of "getting stuck" in speech.

E.g.: "(silence)...I am going".

Multidisciplinary Perspective (5 Specialist Viewpoints)

🧠 Psychological perspective

Stuttering can be comorbid with social anxiety, speech avoidance, and low self-confidence — psychological support complements speech therapy.

✋ Occupational therapist's perspective

General motor coordination and self-regulation skills indirectly support speech motor planning, especially in children with high tension levels.

🌊 Sensory integration perspective

The nervous system's overall arousal level affects speech fluency — calming sensory strategies can be helpful before speaking.

🗣️ Speech-language pathologist's perspective

The primary intervention role — the choice between Fluency Shaping and Stuttering Modification techniques should be tailored to the child's age, severity level, and personal profile.

🏫 Psycho-pedagogical perspective

A teacher giving patient waiting time in the classroom, not rushing the child, reduces academic pressure around stuttering.

4

Presentation by Age Group

👶 Early childhood (ages 2-6)

  • The period when stuttering most often begins, and it often resolves spontaneously
  • The Lidcombe Program is the most effective method for this age group
  • Early intervention significantly reduces the risk of chronic stuttering

📚 School age (ages 7-12)

  • The child is now aware of their stuttering, and secondary behaviors may form
  • A combination of Fluency Shaping and Stuttering Modification is applied
  • Classroom support (informing the teacher) is essential

🎓 Adolescence (13+)

  • Social anxiety and avoidance behaviors are more common
  • Desensitization and self-confidence components take priority
  • The adolescent's own motivation is decisive for the success of intervention

Core Model: An Integrated Approach

This program integrates 3 core, research-supported methods, applied according to the child's age and needs:

🌬️ Fluency Shaping

Diaphragmatic breathing, easy onset, slow speech, and light contact — for building a fluent speech pattern.

🔄 Stuttering Modification (Van Riper)

Pull-out and Cancellation techniques — for managing the moment of stuttering more comfortably.

👨‍👩‍👧 Lidcombe Program

A parent-directed, structured positive-feedback system at home for young children.

💪 Desensitization and Self-Confidence

Reducing fear of stuttering, and strengthening open communication and social skills.

💡 Important note: The choice of method depends on the child's age — for young children (2-6), Lidcombe takes priority; at school age, a combination of Fluency Shaping and Stuttering Modification; and for adolescents, the Desensitization component carries more weight.

Clinical Case: Tural's Story

Tural was 7 years old when his preschool teacher advised his parents that the repetitions in his speech had noticeably increased in recent months. His mother said at the first conversation that they didn't notice it much at home, but at preschool — especially when meeting new children — he would repeat the sound "t-t-t" several times before a word, and sometimes seemed to stumble on the word in his mouth. What struck me most in the first session, honestly, wasn't Tural himself but the family's reaction to him — every time he stumbled, his mother would finish the sentence for him.

Assessment Tool: SSI-4 (Stuttering Severity Instrument)

ℹ️ About the test

Developed by Riley, the most widely used stuttering-severity tool in the world. It scores frequency, duration, and accompanying behaviors (eye blinking, facial tics).

🎯 Why it's given

To determine the objective severity level of stuttering (mild/moderate/severe/very severe) and to plan intervention intensity and program length accordingly.

🎓 Who can administer it

A certified speech-language pathologist; assessment is conducted on both spontaneous speech and a reading sample (for the age group that can read).

🧰 Materials used

Video/audio recorder, stopwatch, standardized reading passage (age-appropriate), assessment sheet, and scoring chart.

Recommendations for Parents

⏳ Give waiting time

Don't rush your child while they're speaking — wait for them to finish their sentence. This is the most important support you can give.

🚫 Avoid saying "talk slower"

This advice can actually create more pressure — instead, model slow speech yourself.

🎯 Praise fluent speech

In line with the Lidcombe principle, positively note sentences that come out smoothly, not the stuttering.

Risk Assessment and Crisis Protocol

⚠️ Situations requiring special attention in the stuttering program

In a stuttering program, the biggest risk isn't the speech itself but the social environment's reaction to it. The following require urgent attention:

  • Sudden refusal to go to school, complete avoidance of speaking in class (signs of a shift toward situational mutism);
  • Physical signs of violence (damaged belongings, unexplained injuries on the body);
  • Expressions of hopelessness ("I'll never be able to speak normally", "I don't want to live");
  • Complete social isolation, withdrawal from peer relationships.
  1. At every session, ask directly and simply about bullying/mockery — children who stutter rarely bring this up themselves, because the sense of shame is strong.
  2. If you encounter language of hopelessness, ask a direct risk question and share it with the parent.
  3. Contact the school administration/school psychologist and request concrete measures to stop the bullying.
  4. In cases of severe emotional distress, refer in parallel to a child psychologist/psychiatrist — speech-language work alone is not sufficient.
  5. Give the parent concrete advice on reducing pressure to "fix" the speech, and instead supporting the child's sense of identity.

📌 Topic Summary

  • Stuttering consists of core behaviors (repetition, prolongation, block) and secondary behaviors, most of developmental origin.
  • The program integrates 3 methods: Fluency Shaping, Stuttering Modification (Van Riper), and the Lidcombe Program for young children.
  • Techniques progress in a hierarchical sequence (sound→word→phrase→sentence→monologue→dialogue→real environment), gradually increasing in complexity.
  • Parent involvement (especially Lidcombe principles) and desensitization (reducing fear) are critical for long-term success.
